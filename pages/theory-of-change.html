Theory of Change

How we get from here to a post-labor world

From Problem to Impact

Our theory of change maps the logical pathway from the problems we're solving, through our activities and outputs, to the outcomes and ultimate impact we seek.

The Problem

What we're solving

AI Displacing Labor

AI, automation, and robotics are eliminating jobs faster than new ones are created, while wages remain the primary income distribution mechanism.

Economic Agency Paradox

Technology lowers costs but destroys the jobs that provide the income to buy cheaper goods—a self-reinforcing spiral.

Laborism

Cultural and political consensus that work is morally necessary prevents serious consideration of alternatives.

Power Vacuum

Without labor leverage, citizens lose the basis of democratic power and social contract negotiation.

Ultimate Impact

The world we're building toward

A Post-Labor World

No one has to work to survive. Contribution is voluntary, not coerced. Income flows from ownership, not wages. Citizens retain democratic power through algorithmic infrastructure. Humanity is free to pursue meaning, creativity, relationships, and flourishing.

Key Assumptions

  • AI capabilities continue advancing at current or accelerating pace
  • Labor displacement is inevitable and accelerating
  • Proven mechanisms (SWFs, UPI, etc.) can scale to new contexts
  • Coalition between workers and forward-thinking capital is possible
  • Policy change can happen proactively, not just reactively
  • Public opinion can shift on laborism with effective education

Key Risks

  • Timing Mismatch: Automation faster than policy adaptation
  • Elite Capture: Benefits of automation hoarded by few
  • Techno-Populist Backlash: Public turns against AI/automation
  • Founder Dependency: Movement too reliant on single leader
  • Economic Collapse: Crisis before transition infrastructure ready
